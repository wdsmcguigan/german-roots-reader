# Skill: Etymological Storytelling Critique

## What This Skill Is For

Use this when reviewing or critiquing content in German Roots Reader to evaluate whether it achieves its core premise: making the English–German linguistic relationship feel alive through anecdote and story, not dry instruction.

## The Book's Core Premise

German Roots Reader is a story-driven etymological exploration. Its appeal is not that it explains *why* German grammar works — it's that it tells *fascinating stories* about where words came from, how sounds shifted, why cognates exist, how meaning drifted, and what that reveals about both languages. The "Why" framing is one useful thread, but the real engine is curiosity and narrative.

**Dual audience:** Every chapter should work for:
1. An English speaker actively learning German (Goethe Institut, TELC, university) who wants conceptual reinforcement
2. A language enthusiast who loves English etymology and has no intention of learning German

If a section only makes sense to someone already studying German, it has narrowed its audience unnecessarily.

## What Good Looks Like

- **Anecdote-first:** Historical stories, surprising origins, counter-intuitive connections come before or alongside the structural explanation
- **Both directions:** The relationship illuminates English *and* German — not just "this helps you remember the German word" but "this changes how you understand the English word too"
- **Conversational curiosity:** Prose sounds like an enthusiastic guide, not a textbook
- **Concrete > abstract:** Specific word histories beat general principles every time
- **Earned tables:** Example blocks appear after the story has made the reader *want* to see the pattern, not as a substitute for explanation

## What to Flag

- Sections that explain a rule without telling a story
- Sections that only make sense if you're actively studying German (learner-only framing)
- Any place the prose sounds like a grammar textbook rather than a fascinating book about language
- Vocabulary lists or tables that appear without narrative context
- Claims like "this will help you remember X" — pedagogical framing that alienates the non-learner audience

## Audiobook Consideration

German Roots Reader has a planned audio/audiobook dimension (and the audiobook may be released independently). When reviewing content, also consider:
- Does this passage work when read aloud? Does the prose have natural spoken rhythm?
- Are visual-only elements (two-column tables, expandable examples) carrying meaning that the prose should also convey?
- Would a listener following along without seeing the screen still get the full value of this section?
- Chapter length: is it appropriate as a listening session (roughly 10–20 minutes of audio)?
