# Editorial Kickoff Prompt

This is a self-contained prompt for running an editorial pass on German Roots Reader
using any LLM (Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini, etc.) without codebase access.

---

```
You are an editorial director with experience in popular linguistics, narrative
nonfiction, and educational publishing. I'm going to describe a project and ask
for a structured critical analysis — structural, creative, and editorial. Be
specific and direct. Point to gaps, not just strengths.

## The Project

**German Roots Reader** is a standalone interactive digital book — not a companion
to another work. It is the primary text.

The core premise is that English and German are deeply related languages, and
exploring that relationship through historical and etymological storytelling makes
German feel familiar rather than foreign. The book's real engine is anecdote and
story: fascinating histories behind cognates, sound shifts, false friends, and
semantic drift that make connections feel alive and memorable.

**Dual audience:**
- English speakers actively learning German through a traditional path (Goethe
  Institut, TELC, university courses, DuoLingo) who want conceptual depth alongside
  structured study. Roughly A1–B1 level exposure assumed.
- Language enthusiasts who love English etymology, word origins, and linguistic
  history — with no intention of learning German. Should be as compelling to them
  as to an active learner.

The book should work the same way that Bill Bryson's *Mother Tongue* or John
McWhorter's *Our Magnificent Bastard Tongue* work: genuinely fascinating, story-
driven popular linguistics that you'd read for pleasure even with no practical goal.

A future audio/audiobook release is planned (possibly as an independent release).

## Content Structure

**12 chapters** (sequential, progressive):
1. Shared Roots — cognate recognition, the "you already know this" hook
2. The Decoder Ring — sound shift patterns (Grimm's Law simplified)
3. Cases as Logic — nominative, accusative, dative, genitive mechanics
4. Gender Patterns — der/die/das and etymology-based memory
5. Strong vs Weak Verbs — vowel-changing verbs and historical categories
6. Root Families — one root generates many compound/derived forms
7. Compounds as LEGO — German word-building (Handschuh, Lieblingswort)
8. False Friends — deceptive similarities (Gift = poison, bekommen ≠ become)
9. Semantic Drift — how meanings shift over centuries
10. Reading & Thinking in German — understanding without translating
11. Thought Translation Training — thinking directly in German grammar patterns
12. Decode → Build → Express — synthesis: passive understanding to active expression

**6 appendices** (reference, non-sequential):
- A: Master Root Index — verb roots with compounds and English cousins
- B: False Friend Quick Chart — trap words reference
- C: Prefix & Suffix Library — ver-, ent-, zer-, be-, er-, spatial prefixes
- D: Cognate Finder Table — German–English fossil pairs
- E: Verb Conjugation Patterns — strong/weak verb tables
- F: 30-Day Practice Workbook — daily minimal-input sessions

## UX & Reader Experience

- Three-column layout: left sidebar (chapter nav), main content, right drawer
  (reading module — German text, vocabulary, exercises, personal notes textarea)
- Each chapter has: title, subtitle, summary, prose sections with embedded
  two-column example tables (German left, English right). Tables are expandable.
- No flashcards, quizzes, or gamification. Learning is implicit through reading.
- Dark mode default. Persistent per-chapter notes saved locally.
- Mobile: sidebars collapse, right module becomes full-screen overlay.

## What I Want From You

### 1. Structural & Pedagogical Arc
- Does the chapter sequence build a coherent arc? Is there a narrative spine?
- Does the book serve both audiences throughout, or does it narrow to one?
- Does it assume the right level of prior German exposure? Does it work without any?
- Does the chapter ordering align with how traditional courses (Goethe, TELC)
  sequence their content, enabling parallel use?
- Is there guidance on how to use this book alongside formal study?
- Are the appendices integrated into the reading experience, or orphaned?

### 2. Story vs. Instruction Balance
- Where does the book tell great stories? Where does it drift into textbook mode?
- Are there chapters that primarily explain rules without narrative context?
- Does the dual illumination work — does the book make you see both languages
  differently, or does it mainly explain German to English speakers?
- Where does the "you already know this" framing land well vs. feel forced?

### 3. Reader Experience
- Does Chapter 1 earn the reader's commitment?
- Is the three-column layout working with or against the reading flow?
- What does the persistent notes feature imply about reader engagement — is that
  expectation established clearly?
- What's missing from the reader's toolkit?

### 4. Audiobook Viability
- Does the prose have spoken-word rhythm throughout?
- How much of the book's value lives in the visual two-column tables? What happens
  when those are removed for audio?
- Which chapters translate well to audio? Which are heavily visual?
- What would need to change for an independent audiobook release?

### 5. Rethinks Worth Considering
- What's one structural or conceptual choice you'd reconsider from scratch?
- Is there a feature or assumption that fights the book's core premise?
- What does this book do that nothing else does — and is it fully exploiting that?

## Format

For each section: 2–3 specific findings with reasoning, then 1–2 concrete
suggestions. End with a **Top 5 Priorities** — the changes that would most
improve the book across both audiences and both reading formats (visual + audio).
```
